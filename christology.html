Christology

Understanding Jesus Christ in Catholic Theology

What is Christology?

Christology is the theological study of Jesus Christ and his nature, role, and significance in Christian faith and theology. The term comes from the Greek words "Christos" (Christ/Anointed One) and "logos" (study or discourse). It is one of the most central disciplines in Christian theology, as the person of Jesus Christ is fundamental to Christian belief.

The Two Natures of Christ

Catholic Christology teaches that Jesus Christ has two natures: divine and human. He is fully God and fully man. This doctrine, known as the Hypostatic Union, was formally defined at the Council of Chalcedon in 451 AD. Jesus' divine nature is co-eternal with God the Father, while his human nature was born of the Virgin Mary. These two natures are united in one person without confusion, without change, without division, and without separation.

Christ's Role in Salvation

Christ is understood to be the redeemer of humanity. Through his incarnation, life, passion, death, and resurrection, Jesus made possible the salvation of all people. Catholics believe that Christ's sacrifice on the cross was the ultimate expression of God's love and the means by which humanity is reconciled with God. His resurrection demonstrates his victory over sin and death, offering hope and eternal life to all believers.

Christ as Lord and Savior

In Catholic theology, Jesus Christ is confessed as Lord (Kyrios) and Savior. His lordship extends over all creation and history. As Savior, Christ offers forgiveness of sins and the grace necessary for human transformation and sanctification. The Catholic faith proclaims that salvation comes through faith in Christ and participation in the sacraments he instituted, particularly Baptism and the Eucharist.

Scripture and Tradition

Catholic Christology is based on sacred Scripture and Sacred Tradition. The Gospels (Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John) present the life and teachings of Jesus, while the epistles of Saint Paul and other apostolic writings develop the theological understanding of Christ's person and work. The early Church Fathers, particularly in the ecumenical councils, provided crucial clarifications on Christological doctrine to counter various heresies and misunderstandings.

Key Christological Councils

Several ecumenical councils addressed Christological questions:

• Council of Nicaea (325) - Affirmed the divinity of the Son and his consubstantiality with the Father
• Council of Constantinople I (381) - Affirmed the full divinity of the Holy Spirit
• Council of Ephesus (431) - Affirmed that Mary is the Mother of God (Theotokos)
• Council of Chalcedon (451) - Defined the two natures of Christ united in one person

Jesus' Incarnation and Humility

The incarnation—God becoming human—is a mystery of humility and love. Though Jesus Christ possessed all divine powers and attributes, he emptied himself (kenosis) to take on human form and experience human life, except sin. His incarnation demonstrates God's desire to be close to humanity and to restore the broken relationship between God and man through his presence among us.
