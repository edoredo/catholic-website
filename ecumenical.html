Ecumenical Councils

The Church's Greatest Assemblies of Bishops

What are Ecumenical Councils?

Ecumenical Councils are assemblies of bishops from the entire Catholic Church gathered to address matters of doctrine, discipline, and the spiritual welfare of the faithful. The term "ecumenical" comes from the Greek word "oikoumene," meaning "the whole inhabited world." These councils represent the universal Church and their decisions carry the highest authority in the Church after Sacred Scripture and Sacred Tradition.

The First Seven Ecumenical Councils

Council of Nicaea I (325) - Addressed the Arian heresy and defined the Nicene Creed, affirming Christ's divinity and consubstantiality with the Father.

Council of Constantinople I (381) - Expanded the Nicene Creed and clarified the doctrine of the Holy Spirit.

Council of Ephesus (431) - Addressed the Nestorian controversy and affirmed Mary as Theotokos (Mother of God).

Council of Chalcedon (451) - Defined the two natures of Christ (divine and human) united in one person.

Council of Constantinople II (553) - Clarified aspects of Christology and addressed the writings of Origen.

Council of Constantinople III (680-681) - Addressed the Monothelite heresy and affirmed Christ's two wills (divine and human).

Council of Nicaea II (787) - Addressed the iconoclastic controversy and affirmed the veneration of holy images.

Medieval and Later Councils

Council of Lateran IV (1215) - Addressed heresy, clerical discipline, and clarified the doctrine of transubstantiation.

Council of Constance (1414-1418) - Addressed schism in the Church and matters of faith and discipline.

Council of Florence (1438-1445) - Attempted reconciliation between the Eastern and Western Churches.

Council of Trent (1545-1563) - The great reforming council that responded to the Protestant Reformation and clarified Catholic doctrine on justification, the sacraments, and other matters.

The First Vatican Council (1869-1870)

Held in Rome, this council addressed the nature of faith, the relationship between faith and reason, and defined the dogma of papal infallibility. It emphasized the Church's unchanging nature and its ability to preserve the deposit of faith in every age.

The Second Vatican Council (1962-1965)

Convened by Pope John XXIII and concluded under Pope Paul VI, Vatican II was one of the most significant councils in Church history. It addressed the Church's relationship with the modern world, promoted liturgical renewal, emphasized the role of laypeople and bishops, encouraged ecumenical dialogue, and renewed the Church's commitment to its ancient traditions in light of contemporary needs.

Purpose and Authority of Councils

Ecumenical Councils serve several purposes: They clarify Church doctrine in response to heresies or misunderstandings, address disciplinary matters affecting the entire Church, and provide guidance for pastoral practice. The authority of ecumenical councils is extraordinarily high—when a council's decrees are confirmed by the Pope, they constitute infallible teaching on matters of faith and morals. The councils represent the living Magisterium of the Church, ensuring that the faith is faithfully preserved and appropriately expressed for each generation.

The Conciliar Process

An ecumenical council typically begins with the Pope's convocation. Bishops from around the world gather, along with theological experts (periti) who assist in the discussions. The council proceeds through various sessions where schemas (draft documents) are debated, amended, and voted upon. Before implementation, the Pope must give his approval. This process ensures that the council's decisions reflect the mind of the universal Church and maintain fidelity to the apostolic tradition.
